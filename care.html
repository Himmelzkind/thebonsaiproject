Care

Introduction

Japanese masters have cultivated the art of bonsai since time immemorial. Bon means pot or vessel and sai means plant or tree. Nevertheless, the bonsai is not a potted plant, but a small tree that loves the open air, wind and weather. Just like in nature, it achieves a considerable lifespan. There are many hundreds of years old bonsais that enjoy religious veneration in Japanese families and are passed down from generation to generation as precious heirlooms.

Location

The Chinese elm is a subtropical plant, so a location outdoors over summer and in a cool room at up to 15 degrees in winter is recommended. If a plant gets enough light, it produces more sugar and oxygen during the day through its leaves than it consumes at the same time to maintain its life processes. Only through this surplus of energy is it able to grow and the stem can become thicker. The plant also tolerates full sun, but then watering must be very intensive.

Watering

The Chinese elm has a rather average water requirement. The soil must never dry out because otherwise the fine roots would die. They should therefore be slightly moist at all times of the year. However, once the soil has become completely dry, place the little tree in water up to the rim of the tray so that it can soak up again properly. When watering, spray the whole plant with rainwater or stale tap water and use a very fine spray so that the soil is not washed out.
